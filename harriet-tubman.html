Chapter One

Early Life & Slavery

Harriet Tubman was born Araminta Ross around 1822 on a plantation in Dorchester County, Maryland. She was one of nine children born to Harriet "Rit" Green and Ben Ross, both enslaved people owned by different planters.

From the time she was a small child, Harriet was hired out to neighboring farms as a domestic servant. She was beaten regularly and suffered greatly under the brutal conditions of slavery. Her spirit, however, proved unbreakable.

Around the age of twelve, Harriet suffered a severe head injury when an overseer threw a two-pound metal weight at another enslaved person and it struck her instead. This injury caused her to suffer from narcolepsy — sudden sleeping spells — for the rest of her life. She later said these spells often came with vivid dreams and visions she believed were messages from God.

Birth Name

Araminta "Minty" Ross — she later took her mother's first name, Harriet, and her husband's surname, Tubman.

Born Into

Slavery in Dorchester County, Maryland — one of the most restrictive slave states in antebellum America.

The Injury That Changed Her

A metal weight caused a traumatic brain injury giving her lifelong narcolepsy. She called the sleeping visions her conversations with God.

Chapter Three

The Underground Railroad

The Underground Railroad was not a real railroad — it was a secret network of safe houses, trails, and brave people who helped enslaved African Americans escape to freedom in the North and Canada. Harriet Tubman became its most famous "conductor."

How She Did It

Tubman traveled only at night, using the North Star as her guide. She carried a gun and warned those she rescued: "You'll be free or die." She used coded songs like "Go Down, Moses" to signal escape routes.

The Routes

Escape routes ran through Maryland, Delaware, and Pennsylvania into the North. Some routes went all the way to Canada after the Fugitive Slave Act of 1850 made even Northern free states dangerous for escaped enslaved people.

The Danger

Slaveholders offered a $40,000 reward for Tubman's capture — equivalent to over $1 million today. Despite this enormous bounty, she was never caught and never lost a single person under her guidance.

Chapter Four

The Civil War Hero

When the Civil War began in 1861, Harriet Tubman joined the Union cause immediately. She served in multiple roles — as a nurse caring for sick and wounded soldiers, as a spy gathering intelligence deep behind Confederate lines, and as an armed scout.

Her most extraordinary achievement came on June 2, 1863. Tubman guided Colonel James Montgomery and 150 Black Union soldiers up the Combahee River in South Carolina. Using intelligence she had gathered from enslaved people in the area, they destroyed Confederate infrastructure and liberated more than 700 enslaved people in a single night.

She became the first woman in American history to lead an armed military raid. Despite her enormous contributions, she received no payment for most of her service and had to fight for a pension for decades.

Combahee River Raid · June 2, 1863

Led 150 Black Union soldiers up the Combahee River. Freed over 700 enslaved people in one night. First woman to lead an armed military raid in U.S. history.

Her Many Roles

Nurse · Spy · Scout · Armed Raid Leader. She served the Union Army in South Carolina, earning the trust of military commanders but no official pay for years.

Chapter Five

Her Lasting Legacy

🗽

Freedom Fighter

Freed approximately 70 people through the Underground Railroad and liberated over 700 more through the Combahee River Raid — a lifetime dedicated entirely to the freedom of others.

✊🏿

Women's Rights Activist

After the Civil War, Tubman devoted herself to the women's suffrage movement, speaking at conventions alongside Susan B. Anthony and Elizabeth Cady Stanton.

🏥

Humanitarian

In her final years, Tubman opened the Harriet Tubman Home for the Aged in Auburn, New York, providing care for elderly African Americans in need.

💵

On the $20 Bill

Harriet Tubman was selected to replace Andrew Jackson on the face of the U.S. $20 bill — a historic recognition of one of America's greatest heroes.
